Data Driven Depression 2

2017

Classroom project at NID

Guides: Chakradhar Saswade and Amit Kapoor

Redesign of the data presented as "Data Driven Depression", a sentiment analysis of radiohead's discography

Brief

This is a reimagining of the dataset published at rcharlie's website that looks at different Radiohead songs and categorises them based on the author's gloom index.

Gloom Index is a calculation of two entities:

1. The song's valence which is a data point from Spotify's algorithm specifying the general mood of the song.

2. The song's lyrical content and sadness which in turn is defined by lyrical density as per an analysis by Myles Harrison and R Charlie's own algorithm to classify words by sadness.

data driven depression

Redesign Strategy

For this dataset, it would be interesting to visualise the number of listeners who have played through each song to contrast the gloom index vs song popularity. Also, for the uninitiated, it would be nice to have a summary of all tracks so that it can enable song discovery for those who might not be familiar with the artist.

For this reason, I chose to add data from two sources:

1. Last.fm is an online catalogue of music that people can listen to for free. It maintains a total play count for all tracks and is easily accessible via a web browser. For an updated count please visit: last.fm's Radiohead page

2. Vulture.com is a website owned by New York (Magazine) which is an American biweekly magazine concerned with life, culture, politics, and style generally, and with a particular emphasis on New York City. Vulture, it's subsidiary did an article on all Radiohead tracks until its publication date giving brief summaries of all of their recorded songs. (Available here)

Process Sketches

I explored various formats as wireframes for the final data visualisation. While in the process, I realised the need to include an embedded music player and a summary of the band's members to add dimensionality to the final product.

A few design decisions need explanation:

1. The data is best visualised as a scatterplot similar to the original rendering on rcharlie's website. This is because some data points are closely spaced and the linear quality of the scatterplot on the album axis identifies songs as being part of the same series and temporality.

2. Tooltips work better than a static pane or sidebar to show additional content as this keeps users from constantly shifting gaze to other corners of the webpage.

3. The identity of the band is as important as the music they produce. For people who do not know of Radiohead, a brief overview of the band members is important to establish the context and creators of the music.
